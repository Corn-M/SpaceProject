Mass (\(M\))

The mass of the star. Mass is estimated with these equations and assumes that the star is on the main sequence.

The units are in M☉, which means mass in relation to the Sun's mass.


Equations: \[M = {L^{1\over4}}\]

Main Sequence Relationship

\[M = ({\tau_☉ \over \tau})^{1 \over 3}\]

Main Sequence Relationship

Radius (\(R\))

The radius of the star.

The units are in R☉, which means radius in relation to the Sun's radius.


Equations: \[R=\sqrt{L \over ({T \over T_☉})^4}\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Temperature (\(T\))

The temperature on the surface of the star. The temperature of the star is directly related to its peak wavelength and determines its color and spectral type (class). It can also be used to estimate bolometric correction.


Equations: \[T = {2.898 \over \lambda_{max}}\]

Wien's Law

\[T = {L^{1\over4}T_☉ \over \sqrt{R}}\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Peak Wavelength (\(\lambda_{max}\))

The peak wavelength is the wavelength in which the star emits the most energy. On a star's black-body curve, which shows the energy output of the star at all wavelengths, the wavelength where the curve's maximum occurs is called the peak wavelength. Peak wavelength is directly related to temperature.


Equations: \[\lambda_{max} = {2.898 \over T}\]

Wien's Law

Luminosity (\(L\))

The luminosity of the star. Luminosity is a measure of the star's total energy output.

The units are in L☉, which means luminosity in relation to the Sun's luminosity.


Equations: \[L = R^2({T \over T_☉})^4\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

\[L = M^4\]

Main Sequence Relationship

\[L = {10^{-{M_V + M_{BC} - M_{V☉} \over 2.5}}}\]

Brightness Ratio

Absolute Visual Magnitude (\(M_V\))

How bright the star's visible light would appear from Earth if it was positioned 10 parsecs (about 33 light years) away from Earth.


Equations: \[M_V = m - 5log_{10}({d \over 10})\]

Distance Modulus

\[M_V = -2.5log_{10}(L) + M_{V☉} - M_{BC}\]

Brightness Ratio

Apparent Magnitude (\(m\))

How bright the star's appears from Earth. This measurement only takes visible light into account, which is light that has a wavelength of about 380 to 700 nanometers.


Equations: \[m = M_V + 5log_{10}({d \over 10})\]

Distance Modulus

Lifetime (\(\tau\))

The period of time between when the star is created (when it starts fusing hydrogen) and when it begins to run out of hydrogen, thus leaving the main sequence and starting to die.


Equations: \[\tau = {\tau_☉ \over M^3}\]

Main Sequence Relationship

Distance from Earth (\(d\))

The distance from Earth to the star.

This calculation is made with trigonometry, using the Earth's orbital diameter as a base.


Equations: \[d = {3.26 \over \theta_{p}}\]

Stellar Parallax

Parallax Angle (\(\theta_{p}\))

Measures (in arcseconds) the change in the star's position in Earth's sky over 6 months.

This method only works up to a certain distance before the parallax angle becomes too small to accurately measure.


Equations: \[\theta_{p} = {3.26 \over d}\]

Stellar Parallax

(\(M_{BC}\))

A correction to convert absolute visual magnitude to bolometric magnitude. Bolometric magnitude is needed to calculate luminosity because it estimates to total amount of energy the star outputs, including wavelengths outside of visible light.

Class/Spectral Type and Color

Stars are classified according to their spectral characteristics, including color, temperature, and luminosity. There are 7 classes of stars (OBAFGKM), with O being the hottest and M being the coolest. Stars that have lower temperatures appear redder and more dim, while hotter stars are more blue and luminous.
