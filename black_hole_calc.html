Mass (\(m\))

The mass of the planet.

The units are in M⊕, which means mass in relation to Earth's mass.


Equations: \[m=\rho r^3{4 \over 3}\pi\]

Mass/Volume

\[m={r^2g \over G}\]

Newton’s Law of Gravity

\[m={4\pi^2 \over G}{{a_{sat}}^3 \over {P_{sat}}^2}\]

Kepler’s Third Law

Radius (\(r\))

The radius of the planet.

The units are in R⊕, which means radius in relation to Earth's radius.


Equations: \[r=\sqrt{D} R\]

Transit Curve

\[r=\sqrt{Gm \over g}\]

Newton’s Law of Gravity

\[r=\sqrt[3]{{m \over \rho}{3 \over 4\pi}}\]

Mass/Volume

Density (\(\rho\))

The density of the planet.

The density is the ratio of mass to volume. It can help astronomers distinguish terrestrial planets from gas and ice giant planets.


Equations: \[\rho={m \over {r^3{4 \over 3}\pi}}\]

Mass/Volume

Surface Gravity (\(g\))

The gravity on the planet's surface.

The greater the surface gravity is, the heavier you'd be on your planet.


Equations: \[g={Gm \over r^2}\]

Newton’s Law of Gravity

Orbital Radius of a Satellite (\(a_{sat}\))

The distance between the center of the ellipse created by the satellite's orbit around the planet and the point on the ellipse furthest from the center. Also called the semi-major axis.

A satellite is any object orbiting the planet (usually a moon).


Equations: \[a_{sat}=\sqrt[3]{{m{P_{sat}}^2}{G \over 4\pi^2}}\]

Kepler’s Third Law

Period of a Satellite (\(P_{sat}\))

The time it takes for the satellite to orbit the planet.

A satellite is any object orbiting the planet (usually a moon).


Equations: \[P_{sat}=\sqrt{{4\pi^2 \over G}{{a_{sat}}^3 \over m}}\]

Kepler’s Third Law

Drop in Star Brightness During Transit (\(D\))

The percentage drop in brightness of the host star as the plant moves in front of it, where the host star is the star the planet orbits around.


Equations: \[D= {r^2 \over R^2}\]

Transit Curve

Mass of Host Star (\(M\))

The mass of the star that the planet orbits.

The units are in M☉, which means mass in relation to sun's mass.


Equations: \[M={4\pi^2 \over G}{{a_{p}}^3 \over P^2}\]

Kepler’s Third Law

Radius of Host Star (\(R\))

The radius of the star that the planet orbits.

The units are in R☉, which means radius in relation to sun's radius.


Equations: \[R= {r \over \sqrt{D}}\]

Transit Curve

\[R=2a_{p}({T \over {T_{s}} ({1 - a})^{1\over4}})^2\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Temperature of Host Star (\(T_{s}\))

The time it takes for the satellite to orbit the planet

A satellite is any object orbiting the planet (usually a moon)


Equations: \[T_{s} = {T \over ({1 - a})^{1\over4}} \sqrt{2a_p \over R}\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Expected Temperature (\(T\))

The estimated temperature (also known as equilibrium or black-body temperature) of the planet.

The expected temperature is different from the actual temperature because it doesn't factor in heating from the atmosphere. The actual temperature will usually be hotter due to gases in the atmosphere trapping heat from the central star, which is called the greenhouse effect.


Equations: \[T = T_{s} ({1 - a})^{1\over4} \sqrt{R \over 2a_p}\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Albedo (\(a\))

The albedo of the planet.

Albedo describes the percentage of light that the planet reflects. Because things like ice, metal, and clouds reflect more light, albedo helps astronomers understand what the planets' surface or atmosphere looks like. Typically, planets with thicker atmospheres have higher albedos due to the increased cloud cover.


Equations: \[a = 1 - (\sqrt{2a_p \over R}{T \over T_{s}})^4\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

Orbital Radius (\(a_{p}\))

The distance between the center of the ellipse created by the planet's orbit around a host star and the point on the ellipse furthest from the center. Also called the semi-major axis.


Equations: \[a_p = R \sqrt{1 - a} {{T_{s}}^2 \over 2T^2}\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

\[a_{p}=\sqrt[3]{{M{P}^2}{G \over 4\pi^2}}\]

Kepler’s Third Law

Orbital Period (\(P\))

The time it takes for a planet to orbit its host star.


Equations: \[P=\sqrt{{4\pi^2 \over G}{{a_{p}}^3 \over M}}\]

Kepler’s Third Law

Distance from Earth (\(d\))

The distance from Earth to the planet.

This calculation is made with trigonometry, using the Earth's orbital diameter as a base, so it will only work for planets outside of our solar system


Equations: \[d = {3.26 \over \theta_{p}}\]

Stellar Parallax

Parallax Angle (\(\theta_{p}\))

Measures (in arcseconds) the change in the planet's position in Earth's sky over 6 months.

This method is only used for planets outside of our solar system.


Equations: \[\theta_{p} = {3.26 \over d}\]

Stellar Parallax

Habitable Zone
(\(a_{hz, inner}\), \(a_{hz, outer}\))

A range describing the distance from the central star where a planet would be able to support life. It's also described as the Goldilocks zone, because it's the perfect temperature, as it's not too hot and not too cold


Equations: \[a_{hz, inner} = 0.75 R ({T_{s} \over T_☉})^2\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law

\[a_{hz, outer} = 1.77 R ({T_{s} \over T_☉})^2\]

Stefan-Boltzmann Law
